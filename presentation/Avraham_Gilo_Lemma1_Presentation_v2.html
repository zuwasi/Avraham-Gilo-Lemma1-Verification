The Paper

Avraham & Gilo (2025)

Insurance Collusion and Imperfect Competition when Insurers Increase Risk

📋 Model Setup

  • W — Initial wealth
  • H — Harm (loss in bad state)
  • P — Accident probability
  • q ≥ 1 — Loading factor (supra-competitive price)
  • α ∈ [0,1] — Coverage chosen by consumer
  • Ra(A) = −U″(A)/U′(A) — Arrow-Pratt coefficient

📐 Lemma 1

  • (i) Single-peaked: If Ra decreases fast enough in wealth, ∃ P̄ that maximizes insurance demand
  • (ii) Monotone decreasing: If Ra is not too decreasing, demand falls monotonically with P
  • Proof via implicit differentiation of the FOC
  • Limit arguments at P→0 and P→1 determine sign of dα*/dP
Proof Step 1

The First-Order Condition

The consumer maximizes expected utility. The FOC equates marginal cost of insurance (premium in the good state) with marginal benefit (coverage in the bad state).

−(1−P)·q·U′(W₁) + (1−qP)·U′(W₂) = 0 where W₁ = W − αqPH (good state) W₂ = W − H(1−α) − αqPH (bad state)

CRRA Specialization: U′(A) = A−γ

(W₂ / W₁)^γ = (1−P)·q / (1−qP) At P = 0: (W − H(1−α))^(−γ) = q · W^(−γ)
Proof Step 2

Optimal Coverage at P → 0

At P→0, the FOC simplifies. Solving for α₀ reveals coverage depends on risk aversion γ.

FOC at P→0: (W − H(1−α))^(−γ) = q · W^(−γ) Solving for α₀: α₀(γ) = 1 − (W/H) · (1 − q^(−1/γ))

🔑 KEY INSIGHT

Coverage α₀ depends on γ — this creates the self-consistency issue later. You cannot compute γ_crit while holding α fixed, because α itself changes with γ.

Proof Step 3

How Coverage Changes with Risk

Implicit differentiation of the FOC yields Eq. (17) — the derivative dα*/dP:

dα*/dP = N / D N = (1−P)·α·q·H·[Ra(W₁) − Ra(W₂)] + (q−1)/(1−qP) D = −(1−P)·H·[qP·Ra(W₁) + (1−qP)·Ra(W₂)] ← always < 0

N < 0 → dα*/dP > 0

Demand rises then falls → Lemma 1(i) single-peaked

N > 0 → dα*/dP < 0

Demand falls monotonically → Lemma 1(ii)

Numerator at P→0

N₀ = α₀·q·H·γ·[1/W − 1/(W − H(1−α₀))] + (q−1)
Key Insight

The Self-Consistency Trap

❌ The Naive Approach (GPT-5's Error)

  • Hold α fixed at some value (e.g. α = 0.5)
  • Compute γ_crit ≈ 5.9
  • Claim Lemma 1(i) holds for γ > 5.9
⚠ WRONG: α itself changes with γ! As γ increases, α₀(γ) → 1 The "fixed α" assumption is invalid.

✅ The Self-Consistent Approach

  • α₀ depends on γ through the FOC
  • As γ ↑, α₀(γ) → 1 (full coverage)
  • Wealth gap W₁ − W₂ vanishes
  • γ_crit(γ) grows faster than γ
Must solve the implicit inequality: γ > γ_crit(γ) where γ_crit depends on α₀(γ).
Proof Step 5

The Asymptotic Argument

Taylor expansion of q−1/γ as γ → ∞ reveals the limiting behavior of the numerator N₀.

Taylor expansion: q^(−1/γ) ≈ 1 − ln(q)/γ as γ → ∞ Then: α₀ → 1 − (W/H)·(ln(q)/γ) The product γ·[1/W − 1/W₂] → −ln(q)/W Therefore: N₀ → (q−1) − q·H·ln(q)/W

Numerical Check: W=100, H=40, q=1.3

N₀ → (1.3 − 1) − 1.3 × 40 × ln(1.3) / 100 = 0.3 − 1.3 × 40 × 0.2624 / 100 = 0.3 − 0.136 = +0.164 > 0

N₀ is ALWAYS positive for typical parameters. Lemma 1(i) NEVER holds for CRRA when H/W = 0.4

Proof Step 6

When CAN Lemma 1(i) Hold?

For N₀ < 0 (single-peaked demand), we need the harm term to dominate:

H/W ≥ (q−1) / (q · ln q)
Loading qMinimum H/WInterpretation
q = 1.10.954Harm ≥ 95.4% of wealth
q = 1.30.880Harm ≥ 88.0% of wealth
q = 1.50.822Harm ≥ 82.2% of wealth

Only catastrophic harm (88%+ of wealth for q = 1.3) produces single-peaked demand with CRRA utility.

Economic Intuition

Why the Two Effects Cancel

✓ Effect 1: Risk Aversion Rises

γ ↑ → Risk aversion increases → Ra(W₁) − Ra(W₂) becomes more negative → pushes N₀ toward negative

✗ Effect 2: Coverage Closes the Gap

γ ↑ → Coverage α₀ → 1 → Wealth gap shrinks → Ra(W₁) ≈ Ra(W₂) → difference vanishes

≈ Net Effect: Forces Cancel

The two effects approximately cancel, leaving the (q−1) > 0 term dominant → N₀ > 0 → Lemma 1(ii)

Utility functions where Ra(A) decreases faster than 1/A (e.g., HARA with appropriate parameters, DRRA class) are needed for Lemma 1(i) to hold at typical harm levels.
